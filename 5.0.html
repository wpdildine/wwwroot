Avoidance / Distraction Return to Top

Children who avoid or appear distracted may be...
  • Overwhelmed by a sound, sight, smell, touch, or taste present in the environment
  • Seeking out sensory input in the form of running, jumping, crashing, etc.
  • Feeling frustrated or confused, for instance, from a challenge during a play session
Solutions
  • If your child seems distracted, raise the level of your emotional energy to entice him to play
  • If your child seems overwhelmed, check to be sure light, sound, smells, etc. are minimized
  • If your child is frustrated, moderate your challenges and be sure you are playful rather than directive
  • If your child seems to need more physical play, offer opportunities for fun, enjoyable chasing, crashing, tickling, etc.
  • Be playfully obstructive (see audio on playful obstruction)

Aimlessness/Inattention Return to Top

Children who appear to be aimless or unable to focus may be...
  • Over stimulated by the environment (too much going on in the room)
  • Having difficulty planning their actions
  • Having difficulty coming up with ideas for what to do next
Solutions
  • If your child seems overwhelmed, get rid of clutter and distractions and make the room feel calm
  • Help your child plan his/her actions by working on step-by-step routines during the day
  • Help your child make decisions by playfully creating a “problem” he/she can “solve” (be a ‘doggy’ that gets in the way, i.e. playful obstruction-listen to audio on playful obstruction)

Revving Up/Hyperactive/Impulsive Return to Top

Children who appear to be over excited or impulsive may...
  • Be over stimulated because too much is going on in their environment
  • Be over stimulated emotionally
  • Be under-stimulated and need more sensory input, such as more movement or firm pressure (e.g. hugs or squishes)
Solutions
  • Help the child with back-and-forth interactions using words and/or gesturing
  • Use language and emotions in an enticing and calming manner so that the child can use that rhythm to calm down (counter-regulate).
  • Provide calm and supportive feedback throughout the day.
  • Set boundaries in a firm but calm and gentle manner

Passivity/Withdrawn Return to Top

Children who appear passive or withdrawn may...
  • Need more sensory stimulation
  • Be responding to caregiver prompts that are directive, which allows the child to be a passive participant
  • Have a hard time initiating Interactions (Opening Circles)
Solutions
  • Offer regularly occurring, high energy/alerting opportunities, such as swinging or spinning
  • Create opportunities for fun, child-led activities.
  • Respond to all small and subtle gestures and communications from your child, and encourage them

Meltdown Return to Top

A child who has meltdowns may be reacting to...
  • An over-stimulating environment (with triggers such as too many people, too many colors or objects, too much noise, etc.)
  • Overly challenging emotional environment, with triggers such as too many demands or challenges
  • Physical Stress (illness, fatigue, allergy) plus a sensory or emotional trigger
Solutions
  • Be very soothing and calming, as you would with an infant.
  • Provide hugs, squeezes and firm pressure (i.e. proprioceptive sensory input) in a slow rhythmic manner while being calm and soothing.
  • Take the child into a calmer or more neutral environment.

Aggression Return to Top

Children who avoid or appear distracted may be...
  • Overwhelmed by emotions and at the same time seeking proprioceptive sensory inputs, such as hitting, kicking, and squeezing. (Their emotions can be stimulated by fatigue, lack of comfort and support, and/or being challenged too much.)
  • Absorbing and expressing, in their own way, impulsive or aggressive behavior they are exposed to by peers or adults.
Solutions
  • Stay calm, but set firm limits. (See audio clips for limit setting.)
  • Only physically restrain a child when they are harming themselves or others.
  • Develop a calmer home environment. (If necessary, parents can seek professional help to deal with their emotions)

Defiance/Rigidity Return to Top

The defiant/rigid child may be...
  • Feeling a lack of control because of the sensory environment . That is, the world around them seems disorganized due to their difficulties in taking in and responding to sensations (i.e. modulating inputs such as noise or touch) and/or making sense of sensations (i.e., processing inputs such as words and gestures).
  • Feeling a lack of control because of their emotional environment, e.g.: many demands are placed on them; they have difficulties in communicating; they are not feeling heard or acknowledged.
Solutions
  • Play sensory games that 1) allow the child to be in control over the sensation (e.g. noise, touch, visual space) through interaction and communication and 2) improve processing through visual, auditory, and planning games
  • Allow child to have regular, daily opportunities to control the interactive activity they choose, especially with the person they are defiant/rigid toward. That is, let them be the boss during fun activities or other reasonable family activities, such as the child giving the tour of the zoo.
  • Continue to follow Floortime principles by challenging and expanding the play.
  • Continue to set boundaries around health and safety.

Self-stimulatory behaviors Return to Top

Children who like to spin, bounce, crash, etc. may be...
  • Seeking sensory inputs on their own to try to calm down.
  • Experiencing stress, either emotional or sensory, and trying to calm themselves.
  • Feeling fatigued
Solutions
  • Provide sensory inputs through an interaction, e.g. if the child is jumping, help them jump higher by holding their hands.
  • Avoid environments that are stressful. If child gets stressed, take the stress (or the child) out of the environment.
  • Keep child well rested and as calm as possible.

Repetitive Actions/Language Return to Top

The child who has repetitive actions/language may have...
  • Difficulties with planning actions
  • Difficulties Expanding play or interactions with new ideas
  • Exposure to being taught actions or language in a rote and repetitive manner
  • Exposure to digital media, especially with emotionally intense language or content (TV, Internet, etc.)
Solutions
  • Help the child plan their own actions and ideas. Try not to direct, show hand over hand, or teach
  • Stop teaching the child language by simply having them repeat the word you or someone else says
  • Join the repetitive activity/language and gradually expand the process of the interaction; don’t direct child to a new activity
