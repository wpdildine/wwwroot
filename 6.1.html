AffectReturn to Top

This is the expression of emotion through facial movement, body language, voice tone. These all convey how we feel inside. ‘Emotion’ is a close but not exact synonym.

AvoidanceReturn to Top

When a child with autism feels overwhelmed, anxious, or uncomfortable, he or she is likely to avoid interaction by physically walking away or by withdrawing attention.

ChallengeReturn to Top

An important element of Floortime involves “challenging” the autistic child by playfully changing or obstructing play. The purpose of challenging is to elicit a direct response – thus “closing a circle of communication.”

Co-regulationReturn to Top

This is a specific kind of interaction between two people who are communicating, where the listener and the speaker continually adjust their tone, facial expression, and words because of the other’s reaction. The communication can also be non-verbal.

DIR® ModelReturn to Top

This stands for Developmental-Individual Differences-Relationship, the basic elements of the developmental model pioneered by Dr. Stanley Greenspan in his seminal 1979 bookIntelligence and Adaptation.

EngagementReturn to Top

Engagement, in the context of Floortime, may be described as two people interacting with one another with interest and pleasure.

ExpandReturn to Top

When a child is responding to an adult’s challenges with back and forth communication, he or she may be ready to expand his or her abilities, skills, or interests. Expanding involves the adult adding to or complicating a play scheme or activity, thus encouraging the child to explore novel movements, activities, ideas, or modes of communication.

Follow the leadReturn to Top

In Floortime, parents and therapists allow the child with autism to take the lead by engaging in preferred activities. It is up to the adults to creatively engage with the child’s interests, even when those interests are unusual.

Functional emotional capacitiesReturn to Top

This refers to the social, emotional, and intellectual capacities that are the focus of the DIR® Model.

Functional Emotional Developmental QuestionnaireReturn to Top

This is the formal name for the Greenspan Assessment for development and sensory processing, which assesses the social, emotional, intellectual, and sensory capacities of the child.

GesturingReturn to Top

Children with autism may or may not have strong verbal skills, but most have the ability to use gestures (movements of the hands, body, or face) to communicate wants, needs, and ideas. Parents and therapists can also use gestures with or without words to open or close circles of communication.

Object-based playReturn to Top

This is a form of play which centers on things (objects) rather than on people – and which does not necessarily assign meaning to the objects. Thus, for example, a child who is running a toy car back and forth on a table without pretending that it is a car (or something else) is engaging in object-based play.

Opening and closing circles of communicationReturn to Top

A “circle of communication” in Floortime consists of back and forth communication in which one person makes a sound, comment, or gesture and their partner responds appropriately. When there are many circles in a row, there is a rhythmical continuous flow of communication. This is the third stage of Greenspan’s DIR® Model.

Playful obstructionReturn to Top

Children with autism often engage in repetitive behaviors. Rather than attempting to change the behaviors, a Floortime therapist or parent may choose to engage with the behavior by playfully obstructing it – thus encouraging the child to respond and thereby close a circle of communication. For example, a child running a car back and forth on a table may respond with a sound, motion, or words when a parent creates a “roadblock” to navigate.

Playing dumbReturn to Top

If an autistic child communicates that, for example, he wants a particular toy, the adult working with him may pretend to misunderstand just what is wanted in order to encourage more back and forth communication. By “playing dumb” (up to but not beyond the point of frustration), the adult may elicit multiple “circles of communication.

Pre-verbalReturn to Top

Many people with autism have significant challenges with verbal communication (talking and listening for meaning). Because many of these individuals do learn to talk over time, however, even children as old as five or six may be referred to as “pre-verbal” rather than “non-verbal.”

ScriptingReturn to Top

Because verbal communication is often challenging for people with autism, it is common for them to use terms or phrases from television or other sources. These “scripts” are an important step toward verbal communication.

Sensory modulationReturn to Top

This is our ability to take in sensory experience—sound, vision, touch, smell, and taste—and movement and filter the experience to create an appropriate response to it. For some of us, certain sensations overwhelm. For others, they underwhelm. In either case, they can affect how we engage with those around us.

Sensory playReturn to Top

This is a form of play in which a child is engaged only through his or her senses, and not through objects or ideas. A child who wants simply to be chased and hugged is engaging in sensory play; a child who wants to be chased and hugged because she is pretending to be a runaway engine is actually engaging in symbolic play (at a higher developmental level).

Sensory regulationReturn to Top

This is the ability to handle sensory input (sights, sounds, smells, tastes, etc.) calmly.

Sensory processingReturn to Top

This is our ability to organize and understand the information we take in through our senses from the environment.

Symbolic playReturn to Top

This is the type of play that is most often associated with pretending or “make believe.” A child who is engaged in symbolic play may pretend that a toy is alive, that a parent is a monster, etc. Children who engage in symbolic play have reached a high developmental level.
